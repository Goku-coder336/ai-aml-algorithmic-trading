# ai-aml-algorithmic-trading
When Algorithms Go Rogue: Whistleblowers &amp; the Hidden Risks of AI-Powered AML in Algorithmic Trading

> 📄 Masters Dissertation | MSc Finance & Economics

---

## Abstract

This dissertation investigates the intersection of artificial intelligence and algorithmic trading, examining how AI-powered systems can be manipulated to facilitate insider trading and financial crime — and how Anti-Money Laundering (AML) systems can be redesigned to better detect and deter such behaviour.

The study analyses the opacity of modern AI trading algorithms, the limitations of existing deterrence strategies (economic and legal), and proposes a framework for more transparent, accountable, and whistleblower-protective AML systems.

---

## Key Research Questions

1. How can AI-powered algorithmic trading be exploited for insider trading and market manipulation?
2. Why do traditional AML monitoring methods fail to detect AI-driven financial crime?
3. How can AI-powered AML systems be redesigned to be more transparent and effective?
4. What role do whistleblowers play in detecting algorithmic misconduct — and how can they be better protected?

## Core Themes

**Algorithmic Complexity & Opacity**
Modern AI trading algorithms are often too complex for regulators and even internal teams to audit fully. This opacity creates opportunities for exploitation — injecting false signals or manipulating market sentiment without triggering traditional surveillance.

**High-Frequency Trading (HFT) Risks**
HFT algorithms can amplify market volatility and trigger flash crashes. Their speed and anonymity make them a prime vehicle for market abuse.

**Explainable AI (XAI) as a Solution**
The dissertation argues that XAI frameworks — which make algorithmic decisions interpretable — are essential for effective AML in automated markets.

**Whistleblower Protection**
A culture of accountability within algorithmic trading firms is proposed as a complementary deterrent alongside technological solutions.

---

## Key Findings

- AI-powered trading creates systemic blind spots in existing AML surveillance
- Economic and legal deterrents alone are insufficient against AI-facilitated market abuse
- Transparent algorithmic design and mandatory back-testing requirements are critical
- Whistleblower protections significantly increase detection rates of internal misconduct
- Regulatory frameworks must evolve in parallel with AI capabilities
